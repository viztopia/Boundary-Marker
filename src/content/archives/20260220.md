## 1) What is a human?

At the most concrete level, a human is a living biological organism: a member of the species *Homo sapiens*—one branch of Earth’s evolutionary tree, with a body that grows, metabolizes, maintains internal balance (homeostasis), reproduces, and evolves across generations. ([Encyclopedia Britannica][1])

But “human” isn’t only a biological label. In everyday life, we also use it as a *moral and social* category: humans are the kinds of beings we assume have inner lives, can suffer, can be wronged, can promise, can be responsible, can belong to communities, can inherit histories, and can make meaning together.

And then there’s the inward-facing angle: many traditions tie being a “person” (not just a biological human) to certain mental qualities—like self-awareness, memory, reasoning, and the ability to see oneself as the subject of experience. Philosophers disagree on the exact list, and some cultures even treat “personhood” as something you can *develop* rather than something automatically granted at birth. ([Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy][2])

So a human is simultaneously:

* **A living organism** (biology),
* **A bearer of dignity and rights** (ethics & law),
* **A meaning-maker in relationship** (culture),
* **A subjective experiencer** (mind).

## 2) What is artificial intelligence?

In contemporary policy and technical language, an AI system is usually defined in functional terms: it’s a **machine-based system** that, for some objective, **infers from inputs how to produce outputs**—like predictions, recommendations, content, or decisions—that can influence physical or digital environments. ([OECD Legal Instruments][3])

Two practical details matter in those definitions:

* **Autonomy varies**: some systems operate with little independence; others can run with minimal human input. ([OECD][4])
* **Adaptiveness may exist**: some systems can change behavior after deployment (e.g., via updates or learning), though many do not. ([OECD][4])

Ethics frameworks often avoid locking AI into one fixed essence—because the technology changes fast—and instead focus on how it should be governed around human rights, dignity, oversight, and harm prevention. ([UNESCO Documents][5])

So, in plain terms: **AI is a class of engineered systems that map inputs to outputs in ways that can look “intelligent,” often by learning patterns from data—without being alive in the biological sense.**

## 3) Where is the boundary between humans and artificial intelligence?

The boundary isn’t one line. It’s more like a *stack* of boundaries, and different societies emphasize different layers.

### A. The biological boundary: living vs. engineered

Humans are alive—self-maintaining organisms produced by evolution; AI systems are artifacts—built, deployed, and maintained. The difference here is not “smart vs. not smart,” but **organism vs. machine**. ([National Academies][6])

### B. The experiential boundary: having an inner life vs. simulating one

Humans don’t just behave; we *feel like something from the inside* (pain, longing, embarrassment, awe). A lot of philosophy and neuroscience treats conscious experience as bound up with being a subject of experience. ([Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy][7])
Today’s AI can produce convincing language about feelings, but whether it *has* feelings is a different claim—and one we don’t currently have a settled scientific test for.

### C. The agency boundary: responsibility and accountability

Humans are treated as responsible agents: we can owe explanations, be blamed or forgiven, make commitments, and be held to norms. AI systems can generate outputs that *affect* the world, but policy frameworks tend to keep **accountability on the humans and organizations** that build and deploy them, emphasizing oversight. ([UNESCO][8])

### D. The personhood boundary: who counts as a “someone”?

This is the most contested layer. Some traditions tie personhood to psychological capacities; others tie it to community recognition or moral development. ([Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy][2])
That means the “boundary” is partly a mirror: it reflects what a civilization believes a person *is*—and what it believes deserves protection regardless of utility.

### E. The practical boundary: what roles should be delegated?

Even if an AI becomes extremely capable, societies may still draw firm lines around:

* coercive power (policing, sentencing),
* intimate care (therapy, caregiving decisions),
* political authority,
* identity-defining relationships,
  because these areas hinge on dignity, trust, and legitimacy—values emphasized in global AI ethics efforts. ([UNESCO][8])

**A useful way to say it:**

* Humans are *living subjects* embedded in biology, history, and relationships.
* AI is *engineered capability*—systems that can imitate or exceed parts of human cognition in output, while lacking (so far) the clear markers of being alive, having lived experience, and being morally accountable in the way we hold persons accountable. ([OECD Legal Instruments][3])
