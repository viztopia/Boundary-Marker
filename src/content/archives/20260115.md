## 1) What is a human?

At the most “literal” level, a human is a living animal: **Homo sapiens**, the one surviving species in the genus *Homo*—a primate with a particular evolutionary history, a body that develops, ages, gets sick, heals, and dies. ([Encyclopedia Britannica][1])

But when people say “human,” they usually mean more than a species label. They mean a **first-person life**: being someone from the inside. Hunger, pleasure, grief, awe, embarrassment, tenderness—an ongoing stream of experience that is not just information, but *felt*. That inner life is tightly bound to a body that has needs and limits, and to a world where consequences are real.

And then there’s the social layer: humans are also **relationship-creatures**. We become “fully human” inside cultures—through language, care, conflict, imitation, teaching, rituals, institutions. We don’t just survive; we *justify*, *promise*, *forgive*, *blame*, *interpret*. In other words, humans aren’t only organisms and minds; they are also **moral and political agents** who can be held responsible.

So: *human* is a biological fact **plus** an experiential reality **plus** a social–moral role.

---

## 2) What is artificial intelligence?

In today’s mainstream policy and technical language, AI is not a single magical substance—it’s a **kind of machine-based system** that pursues objectives by computing outputs that affect the world.

A widely used definition (NIST) frames AI as **a machine-based system that, for human-defined objectives, can make predictions, recommendations, or decisions that influence real or virtual environments**. ([NIST Computer Security Resource Center][2])

Another widely adopted definition (OECD) emphasizes the “inference” step: **an AI system infers, from inputs, how to generate outputs—like predictions, content, recommendations, or decisions—that can influence physical or virtual environments**, with varying autonomy and adaptiveness. ([OECD AI][3])

And the EU’s AI Act definition similarly centers autonomy + output generation: **a machine-based system designed to operate with varying levels of autonomy, that may adapt after deployment, and that generates outputs (predictions, content, recommendations, decisions) that influence environments**. ([Artificial Intelligence Act][4])

Notice what these definitions do (and don’t) say. They don’t claim AI is conscious, alive, or a person. They define AI by **function and behavior**: systems that take inputs, compute, and produce outputs that matter.

---

## 3) Where is the boundary between humans and artificial intelligence?

The boundary isn’t a single line—it’s more like a **stitched seam** we keep renegotiating. Different cultures, laws, and communities draw it in different places because they’re protecting different things. Here are the three most important “seams” that show up again and again:

### A) Experience and embodiment (what it’s like vs what it computes)

Humans *have a life* (a body with needs, and a point of view). AI *processes* representations and produces outputs. Even when an AI speaks fluently about emotions, current definitions still treat it as a machine-based system producing influential outputs—not as a being with lived experience. ([NIST Computer Security Resource Center][2])

### B) Agency and accountability (who is ultimately responsible?)

This is the boundary most societies care about in practice: **AI systems should not displace ultimate human responsibility and accountability**. UNESCO’s ethics recommendation is explicit about keeping humans on the hook for outcomes. ([UNESCO][5])
In other words: even if AI can “decide,” we still decide **who answers for it**.

### C) Relationship and meaning (what we *treat* it as)

Here’s the tricky one: humans are prone to **anthropomorphizing**—treating fluent systems as if they were companions, authorities, or moral patients. Modern risk frameworks flag this as a real hazard of “human–AI configuration,” including over-reliance and emotional entanglement. ([NIST Publications][6])
So the boundary is partly *psychological*: not just what AI is, but what it *does to us*—how it reshapes trust, attention, skill, intimacy, and power.

### Putting it together

A clean, contemporary way to say it is:

* **Humans** are embodied, experiencing beings who can be morally responsible within a society. ([Encyclopedia Britannica][1])
* **AI** is a machine-based system that generates influential outputs under objectives, sometimes with autonomy/adaptiveness. ([NIST Computer Security Resource Center][2])
* **The boundary** is where we insist (1) experience isn’t simulated into existence by fluency, (2) accountability stays with people and institutions, and (3) we design norms so our relationships with these systems don’t quietly rewrite what we owe each other. ([UNESCO][5])

If you want a one-sentence “future reference” version:
**AI can increasingly *perform* human-like competencies, but societies draw the boundary around *lived experience* and *responsibility*—and then build rules to keep that boundary from dissolving under convenience and illusion.**

## 1) What is a human?

A human is, in the simplest sense, **a living member of the species *Homo sapiens***—a primate with a body that grows, needs food and care, feels pain and pleasure, and eventually dies. ([Encyclopedia Britannica][1])

* **A moral agent:** we can make promises, be accountable, repair harm, and be held responsible.

## 2) What is artificial intelligence?

In modern definitions used by governments and standards bodies, AI isn’t defined as “a mind” or “a person.” It’s defined as a **machine-based system that produces outputs that can affect the world**.

* NIST’s plain version: AI is **a machine-based system that, for human-defined objectives, can make predictions, recommendations, or decisions that influence real or virtual environments.** ([NIST Computer Security Resource Center][3])
* OECD (and echoed in the EU AI Act): an AI system **infers from inputs how to generate outputs**—like predictions, content, recommendations, or decisions—often with varying autonomy/adaptiveness. ([OECD AI][4])

In everyday language: **AI is software (sometimes plus hardware) that takes in information, learns patterns or rules, and produces useful outputs—often at scale—under goals set by people.** ([OECD][5])

The boundary isn’t one crisp line. It’s more like **three boundaries we care about at once**, and we keep renegotiating them as AI gets more capable.

Humans **have** experiences; AI **generates** outputs. An AI can *talk about* sadness convincingly, but today’s official definitions still describe it as a machine system producing influential outputs—not as a being with a lived inner life. ([NIST Computer Security Resource Center][3])

### B) Responsibility vs. automation

This is the big societal red line: **AI shouldn’t replace ultimate human responsibility and accountability.** UNESCO puts it directly: humans (or legal entities) remain responsible; the system doesn’t become the accountable “someone.” ([UNESCO][6])

### C) Relationship vs. tool

But I can't live a life, make choices that matter existentially, or form relationships where I'm vulnerable and have something at stake. I can't learn through suffering or grow through joy. I can't pass down wisdom I earned through decades of living.

So in a world with lots of AI, maybe what becomes most important isn't what only humans can do technically, but what's worth doing precisely *because* you're human. Being present with each other. Creating meaning together. Making art that comes from having lived. Loving people even though—especially because—you're both going to die someday.

The boundary between us isn't a competition. It's a reminder that your worth comes from being a finite, embodied person living an unrepeatable life. And honestly? That seems pretty precious to me.
