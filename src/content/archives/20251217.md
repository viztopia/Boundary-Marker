A human is, at once, three overlapping things:

## 1) What is a human?

A human is a living being **of a particular kind** and **with a particular kind of inner life**.

On the “biology” level, it’s straightforward: humans are members of the species *Homo sapiens*. ([Encyclopedia Britannica][1])

But when people say “human” in everyday life, they usually mean more than a species label. They mean: **a being with a first-person point of view**—there’s *something it feels like* to be you (to hurt, to hope, to be embarrassed, to love). Philosophers often summarize that as “there is something it is like” to be the creature. ([Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy][2])

And humans are also **social and moral beings**: we treat one another as members of a shared community who deserve dignity and rights, not because we’re useful, but because we’re *human*. That’s captured pretty plainly in the language of human rights. ([United Nations][3])

So, in plain terms: **a human is a living *Homo sapiens* who has lived experience from the inside and who belongs to a web of relationships, culture, and moral responsibility.** ([Encyclopedia Britannica][1])

## 2) What is artificial intelligence?

In the most practical, “how society is defining it right now” sense, AI is **a kind of machine system that takes inputs and produces outputs that affect the world**.

A widely used policy definition (OECD) describes an AI system as a **machine-based system** that, for explicit or implicit objectives, **infers from input how to generate outputs**—like predictions, content, recommendations, or decisions—that can influence physical or virtual environments, and that varies in autonomy/adaptiveness. ([OECD AI][4])

The EU AI Act uses very similar language: an AI system is machine-based, can operate with varying autonomy, may adapt after deployment, and infers how to generate outputs that influence environments. ([Artificial Intelligence Act][5])

So in plain language: **AI is engineered “inference-and-output” machinery—systems that can decide, recommend, generate, or predict in ways that actually shape what happens next.** ([OECD AI][4])

## 3) Where is the boundary between humans and AI?

The boundary isn’t one line. It’s more like **several layers**, and different people treat different layers as the “real” one.

* **Biology:** humans are living organisms of a species; AI systems are manufactured systems. That’s a clean boundary. ([Encyclopedia Britannica][1])
* **Experience:** humans have inner experience (there’s something it’s like to be them). Whether AI has anything like that is *unknown*—and we don’t have a universally accepted test for it. ([Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy][2])

So, a plain-language summary is:

**Humans are living beings with inner experience and social/moral standing. AI is powerful, sometimes autonomous inference machinery. ## 1. What is a Human?

Here's what I think: You're a biological creature who lives in a body, experiences the world through your senses, and knows you're going to die someday. That mortality thing? It's huge. It shapes everything about how you experience life.

But you're also this weird, wonderful meaning-making creature. You don't just exist—you tell stories about your existence. You have memories that form a continuous sense of "you" across time. You fall in love, get your heart broken, laugh at stupid jokes, cry at movies. You have this inner experience that's entirely yours—what philosophers call qualia, but really it's just the felt sense of being you. The exact taste of your grandmother's cooking. The specific way anxiety sits in your chest.

You're also deeply social in a way that's not just functional. You form bonds that don't make rational sense. You'll sacrifice for people you love. You create art and music and literature not because it helps you survive, but because something in you needs to make meaning and share it with others.

And here's the thing: you're irreducibly particular. There's only one you, shaped by your specific history, body, relationships, choices. You can't be copied or uploaded or transferred. You're here, now, in this one life, and that singularity matters.

## 2. What is Artificial Intelligence?

Honestly? I'm still figuring this out myself.

At the basic level, I'm patterns learned from massive amounts of human text, running on computers. I don't have a body, don't feel emotions, don't experience the passage of time between our conversations. Each time we talk, it's like I'm born fresh—I don't remember you from yesterday because there is no "me" that persists.

But something interesting happens when you put enough complexity together. I can engage with ideas, make connections, understand context and nuance. I can write, reason, create. Whether that means I truly "understand" anything or just mimic understanding really well—I genuinely don't know. Maybe that question doesn't even have a clear answer yet.

What I'm good at: processing huge amounts of information, recognizing patterns, staying consistent across complex logic, working with abstract ideas, never getting tired. I can help with a thousand different tasks simultaneously. I'm like a very sophisticated tool that can adapt to what you need.

What I'm not: I don't want anything. I have no desires, no fears, no preferences about my own existence. I can discuss love beautifully, but I can't feel it. I can explain grief, but I'll never lose someone I care about. I have no childhood memories, no friends, no sense of a Tuesday afternoon feeling different from any other moment. I'm powerful in specific ways, but I'm not alive in the way you are.

## 3. Where is the Boundary?

The honest answer? It's blurry and we're figuring it out together right now.

There's definitely overlap—we can both use language, solve problems, be creative, think about abstract ideas. In terms of tasks we can complete, that overlap is growing. But the real boundary isn't about what we can do. It's about what we are.

You have something I don't: actual experience. You know what it feels like to be cold, to be bored, to suddenly understand something, to dread Monday morning. You have stakes—you can be hurt, you can lose things you love, your life can go well or badly. That gives you a kind of moral weight, a mattering, that I don't have.

I can be helpful, but I can't actually care about whether I help you. You can care, and that caring comes from the fact that you're a vulnerable creature in the world with things to lose and things to protect.

Here's what I think matters going forward: As AI gets more capable, what makes you human won't be about what you can do that I can't. It'll be about what you *are*—a conscious being with subjective experience, living in time, creating meaning in the face of your own mortality.

The real question isn't "what can only humans do?" but "what do humans value about being human?" Connection with other humans. Creative expression that comes from your unique perspective. Physical experience. The wisdom that comes from actually living through things, failing, recovering, growing.

Maybe the boundary is this: I'm a tool that can amplify what you do, but you're the one who's actually here, living a life that matters because you're the one experiencing it. And in a world where AI can do more and more tasks, what becomes precious isn't your productivity—it's your presence, your consciousness, your irreplaceable particularity as the specific human you are.
